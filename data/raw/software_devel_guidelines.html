1 Executive summary  
Software is a central component of academic research and is developed and used in the various  
institutes at Forschungszentrum Jülich. Software refers to all forms of program code and executable  
programs generated from it that are developed, provided, and distributed within the scope of  
activities at Forschungszentrum Jülich.  
Until now, Forschungszentrum Jülich did not have a practical or secure framework within which  
employees could develop software. With these guidelines, Forschungszentrum Jülich now presents  
a document that aims to support the development, management, and impact of software that meets  
high quality standards. These guidelines are primarily aimed at all software developers and senior  
employees who are directly or indirectly involved in the development, management, and distribution  
of software at Forschungszentrum Jülich. The objective of the guidelines is to establish a tangible  
and sustainable use of programmed software at Forschungszentrum Jülich, to improve software  
quality, and to increase the appreciation of high-quality software. The professionalization in the field  
of software development thus generated will help to achieve a sustainable use of software and  
promote good scientific practice in terms of verifiability and reproducibility of research results.  
In developing the guidelines, both internal framework conditions (e.g. the Regulations for Upholding  
Good Scientific Practice at Forschungszentrum Jülich GmbH, the internal provisions of the directive  
“Publications by Forschungszentrum Jülich GmbH”, the “Guidelines for Handling Research Data at  
Forschungszentrum Jülich”, and the “Forschung und Innovation 2025” innovation strategy) and  
external recommendations (e.g. the “Checklist to Support the Helmholtz Centers in Implementing  
Policies on Sustainable Research Software” of the Helmholtz Association’s Open Science Working  
Group) were taken into account.  
The draft is based on the “Model Policy on Sustainable Software at the Helmholtz Centers” of the  
Helmholtz Association’s Open Science Working Group, which has been adapted to the conditions  
and requirements of Forschungszentrum Jülich.  
In the guidelines, software or software projects are classified according to four different application  
classes. These classes range from application class 0 (software that is only used within your team  
or within Forschungszentrum Jülich) to application class 3 (software that is licensed to third parties  
and has product characteristics). Depending on the respective application class, certain minimum  
requirements and measures, which mainly relate to software development and documentation, are  
recommended to enable high standards in software development, software quality, and  
management.  
If the provision or distribution of the software is desired or required, the software developers, the  
project managers, and/or the institute heads will agree on the appropriate transfer path. There is the  
option of either making the software openly available via an open source licence or distributing it to  
third parties via a proprietary licence. If distribution via an open source licence is desired, the  
respective institute is responsible for selecting the appropriate open source licence. If economic  
exploitation or a restriction of the rights of use and thus proprietary licensing of the software to third  
parties is desired, the institute must consult with Corporate Development – Innovation and Strategy  
(UE-I), which is responsible for drafting and negotiating any proprietary licensing agreements.  
Forschungszentrum Jülich supports the provision and distribution of software developed at  
Forschungszentrum Jülich regardless of whether the code is shared under a proprietary licence (i.e.  
for restricted use) or open source. When published as open source software, the reach and transfer  
effect is enormous, as millions of developers are active on platforms such as GitHub. For this reason,  
and to fulfil the criterion of sustainability in the science system, Forschungszentrum Jülich  
encourages researchers to make software freely accessible as “open source” software.  
For the distribution or provision, legal aspects, possible funding conditions, suitable transfer paths,  
and approval processes, etc. must be taken into account. A checklist (Annex A) and a decision tree  
(Annex B) are available to help with the decision. In addition, the advisory services of Corporate  
Development (UE), Law and Patents (R), and the Central Library (ZB) can be used if required. In  
future, the decision-making aids and advisory services provided by Forschungszentrum Jülich will  
be made available centrally via the newly established RSE portal. This portal is jointly moderated by  
UE, R, and ZB and its content is created together with the software developers. Further information  
such as best-practice examples, FAQs, contact persons, as well as announcements concerning

2 Introduction  
Software is developed and used in almost all institutes of Forschungszentrum Jülich. In these  
guidelines, software refers to all forms of program code (e.g. source code and associated  
documentation) and executable programs generated from these, which are developed, provided,  
and distributed within the scope of activities at Forschungszentrum Jülich. The development of  
software is part of a creative process and thus generates executable knowledge. It is an integral part  
of modern publication contexts consisting of written publication, data sets, and software.  
Furthermore, software development is an intellectual achievement protected by copyright and, in the  
context of research, an independent product of scientific work.  
Most scientists who write code are not trained software developers and often orient themselves to  
their immediate working environment and the experiences of their colleagues when it comes to  
software engineering, without necessarily knowing the institution’s advisory services, tools, and best-  
practice experiences.  
In order to support these scientists in their independence and ability to act, these guidelines provide  
a framework for the development, management, and distribution of software at Forschungszentrum  
Jülich. The guidelines establish basic requirements for the development, management, and  
distribution of software, and describe practical procedures as well as corresponding examples and  
advisory services.  
The guidelines deal with the software life cycle [1], from software development and documentation  
to software distribution and maintenance. The guidelines aim to support the establishment of modern  
software engineering methods at Forschungszentrum Jülich, which enable high standards in  
software development, software quality, and management. This professionalization will help to  
achieve more sustainability and promote good scientific practice in terms of verifiability and  
reproducibility of research results.  
These guidelines are primarily aimed at all developers and senior employees who are directly or  
indirectly involved in the development, management, and distribution of software at  
Forschungszentrum Jülich. The objective of the guidelines is to establish a tangible and sustainable  
use of programmed software at Forschungszentrum Jülich, to improve software quality, and to  
increase the appreciation of high-quality software.  
The guidelines take into account the Helmholtz Association’s perspective on open science and are  
based on the “Model Policy on Sustainable Software at the Helmholtz Centers” [2] of the Helmholtz  
Association’s Open Science Working Group. They reflect and implement the Regulations for  
Upholding Good Scientific Practice at Forschungszentrum Jülich GmbH [3], the internal provisions  
of the directive “Publications by Forschungszentrum Jülich GmbH” [4], the “Guidelines for Handling  
Research Data at Forschungszentrum Jülich” [5], and the directive on information security (in  
preparation). The guidelines also take into account the challenges and fields of action related to  
Forschungszentrum Jülich’s innovation strategy [6]. In addition, the “Checklist to Support the

In the spirit of open science, access to and reuse of software are the basis for traceability, verifiability,  
and reproducibility of scientific results. In parallel to the FAIR principles [8] (findable, accessible,  
interoperable, reusable), which apply to research data, Forschungszentrum Jülich also applies these  
sustainability principles to software. To fulfil the criterion of sustainability in the science system,  
Forschungszentrum Jülich encourages researchers to make software openly accessible as “open  
source” software. At the start of a software project, however, the institute developing the software  
must consider the transfer path that will have the most effective impact on society and the economy.  
If there are commercial applications for the software, economic exploitation using proprietary  
licences may make more sense than an open source publication. Proprietary licences can also drive  
innovation (e.g. in a company) and secure a technological edge (e.g. for a spin-off). Open science,  
on the one hand, and commercial exploitation, on the other hand, are not contradictory, but  
complementary components of Forschungszentrum Jülich’s innovation strategy.  
3 Principles for the development, documentation, and distribution of  
software at Forschungszentrum Jülich  
Software and software projects differ from each other both in terms of complexity and maturity. The  
complexity of a software project can result from technical aspects but also from management  
aspects, such as joint software development in a large consortium. In addition, the scientific field,  
the programming technologies used, the target group of the project, and the potential risk (e.g.  
liability in case of malfunction/failure) that may be associated with the final software product must be  
taken into account. In practice, software development is an iterative and ongoing activity that  
changes throughout the software life cycle. The measures and quality standards regarding software  
quality, which are established by Forschungszentrum Jülich, are based on the application class of  
the software developed. At the beginning of a project, those responsible assign each software  
developed to an application class (AC) for which the measures described regarding quality and  
documentation must be adhered to. As experience has shown that many projects start small but then  
develop further, it is recommended to use standards of higher application classes from the very  
beginning. These application classes are based on the application classes proposed and  
implemented by the German Aerospace Center (DLR) [9]. The quality requirements for software  
engineering increase with each application class.  
3.1 Classification according to application classes  
The application classes depend primarily on the intended use of the software and on whether and to  
what extent it will be distributed. Planned or required distribution of the software is a decisive criterion  
for the selection of the application class. However, the measures and standards for the  
corresponding application class mainly relate to software development and documentation. The  
complexity of a software project and the maturity of the software are therefore not necessarily  
reflected in the application classes. It is possible to switch from one application class to another but  
usually from lower to higher classes. It can therefore be helpful to orient yourself to the standards of  
the higher application class right from the beginning, if software distribution is not excluded. The  
necessary use of resources, which must be ensured at the institute throughout the entire software  
life cycle, must be taken into account.

3.2 Minimum standards and measures for the application classes  
Application class 0 and higher:  
1. Consideration of legal aspects (e.g. third-party rights) is an integral part of the software  
development process and requires appropriate documentation (e.g. copyright notices). The  
checklist in Annex A provides assistance on this topic.  
Example: If programming is based on existing software or if libraries are used, these licensing  
conditions must be observed.  
The use of a version control system is recommended.  
Additionally for application class 1 and higher:  
2. Version control systems and code repositories are used that contain or refer to all  
components of the software required for its use, and that are used for change management.  
Example: Any changes to the source code are documented using Git.

3. A short, meaningful description of the purpose of the software must be included (e.g. in the  
form of a README file in the repository). New contributors should be able to participate in  
the development of the software.  
Example: Explanation of the scientific context of the software, reference to publications and  
parties involved as well as technical information on use and further development. This makes  
access easier for new contributors in particular.  
4. To ensure citability, metadata must be attached in a machine-readable format as citation  
information[10]].  
Example: The “Citation File Format” and “CodeMeta” allow an indexing and rendering of  
citation information on platforms such as GitHub.  
5. The tools and standards for software development, validation, and verification must be  
agreed upon with the development team (and superiors) and their use must be documented.  
Wherever possible, software development is carried out according to recognized standards  
and using state-of-the-art tools during development, validation, verification, and provision.  
Example: Tools like SoftWipe https://github.com/adrianzap/softwipe and publications such as  
“Best Practice for Scientific Computing” (Wilson et al. 2014; PLOS Vol.12 Issue 1). More best-  
practice examples, tools, and publications are listed in the RSE portal (see Section4).  
6. Releases must be clearly labelled for users, for example by using release numbers. For large  
software projects, defining a joint release policy is recommended.  
Example: To ensure the reproducibility of results and communication, Git “tags” are extremely  
helpful, especially in combination with a persistent identifier.  
7. To make it possible to refer to or cite software, releases (and, if applicable, the software as  
such) must be assigned a persistent identifier (PID). The necessary metadata can be found  
in the citation information (see above).  
Example: Even software that is not publicly accessible can be FAIR and receive a DOI, for  
example, which is easier and more commonly used in text publications. In addition, a DOI  
allows for subsequent moving without breaking the reference. Reproducibility is greatly  
facilitated by citable releases of data, software, and environment.  
8. Each software publication of Forschungszentrum Jülich must be documented analogously to  
data publications in the institutional repository, at least with metadata. Unless a publication  
is produced, software should be made discoverable in a central internal software catalogue.  
Additionally for application class 2 and higher:  
9. Appropriate installation, development, and application documentation is provided for  
distributing and using the software. The documentation should be easily understood by  
others to facilitate contributions from other contributors.  
10. Functional tests of the software are carried out systematically. For this purpose, an adequate  
test strategy is defined and documented.  
11. If the software is developed as part of a cooperation, the conditions for grants as well as the  
provisions of the contracts or grant agreements and/or the provisions of the consortium must  
be observed.  
If individual developers of Forschungszentrum Jülich contribute code to software projects of  
third parties, a contributor licence agreement (CLA) must be concluded if no cooperation  
agreement has been concluded. A sample CLA is available in the RSE portal.  
12. Before the software is distributed to third parties outside of Forschungszentrum Jülich, a  
licence for the software is established that regulates the use of the software by third parties.  
Licensing agreements for third-party software used are complied with. The software is not  
distributed without a licence. A copyright notice is required for distribution.

3.3 Provision, distribution, and citation  
Forschungszentrum Jülich supports the provision and distribution of software developed at  
Forschungszentrum Jülich regardless of whether the software is distributed in source code or object  
code and regardless of whether the code is shared under a proprietary licence (i.e. for restricted use)  
or open source. When published as open source software, the reach and transfer effect is enormous,  
as millions of developers are active on platforms such as GitHub. For this reason and to fulfil the  
criterion of sustainability in the science system, Forschungszentrum Jülich encourages researchers  
to make software openly accessible as “open source” software (see Section 4.3).  
According to the above assignment of software to application classes, software assigned to the  
application classes 2 and 3 is suitable for distribution. A distinction is made as to whether software  
is distributed in the form of a freely accessible publication (e.g. via data repositories, journals, and  
archives) or within the framework of a licence that grants the user rights of use for the software.  
Any provision of software by employees of Forschungszentrum Jülich must comply with the approval  
process of the institute that the employee belongs to. The approval process is independently

The following applies to software citation:  
In accordance with the DFG Guidelines for Safeguarding Good Research Practice  
Forschungszentrum Jülich calls upon its employees to disclose whether their own scientific software  
or that of others has been used by referring to or citing it in their publications.  
Referring to and citing the software used is key when it comes to ensuring reproducibility of results  
and visibility of non-scientific employees. Ideally, FAIR software publications with persistent  
identifiers are available, from which the necessary data can usually be taken. If they are not available,  
citation information may be available in the code repository (see Section 3.2).  
Irrespective of its public accessibility, software must be cited in a scientific publication using the  
following information [11], [12]:  
Author(s), name of the software, PID, publication date, [release number, if available]  
If no PID has been assigned, a revision and the URL of the source text repository (or a qualified  
software archive such as the Software Heritage archive [13]) can be provided instead.  
The following applies to the provision and distribution of software:  
1. The software was created according to the standards of the respective application class, and  
is well documented and versioned.  
2. Aspects related to (copyright) law, potential third-party rights, funding requirements, and  
approval processes must be taken into account for the distribution and publication of the  
software.  
3. Before software is provided and distributed to third parties, the desired transfer path (open  
source/proprietary licence) should be defined.  
Important points to be considered in this context include the degree of openness and access  
granted, potential commercial exploitation projects, and any (legal) restrictions. This  
constitutes the basis for the decision on the transfer path. If necessary, the advisory services  
and decision-making aids mentioned in Section 4.1 can be used.  
4. Whenever possible, developers make the software, on which the publication is based,  
accessible in recognized archives and repositories [14].  
5. Irrespective of the accessibility of the software as such, metadata on the software must be  
published in the sense of a software publication.  
6. Regardless of the type of provision and distribution, whether it be freely accessible or  
proprietary, the user must be granted the corresponding rights of use by means of a licence  
(see Section 4.3).  
7. When granting rights to use software developed at Forschungszentrum Jülich, regardless of  
whether the software is distributed under an open source or proprietary licence, the copyright  
is usually indicated as follows:

4 Support and services at Forschungszentrum Jülich  
In order to support the development of software at Jülich that meets high quality standards,  
Forschungszentrum Jülich provides advisory, support, and training options. In this context, advice  
and information refer to all phases of the software life cycle and software distribution. The various  
services with regard to software are documented centrally as well as in detail and updated regularly  
in the RSE portal . The portal is jointly moderated by Corporate Development (UE), Law and Patents  
(R), and the Central Library (ZB) and its content is created together with the software developers.  
4.1 Advisory services and decision-making aids  
In case of questions and uncertainties in dealing with self-programmed software, a personal advisory  
session can be arranged with the relevant contact persons at UE, R, and/or ZB, whose up-to-date  
contact details can be found in the RSE portal . During the advisory sessions, issues are addressed  
such as the desired impact of the software, suitable transfer paths or publication channels (e.g. open  
source software or proprietary software), and existing legal framework conditions (with regard to  
copyright law) that must be taken into account. If proprietary licensing of the software is desired (see  
Section 4.5), the framework conditions will be clarified in an advisory session and a suitable licensing  
agreement will be drawn up.  
In addition to personal advisory sessions, practical support is available in the form of information and  
tools in the RSE portal. A checklist (Appendix A) and a decision tree (Appendix B) can help to clarify  
unresolved issues and important aspects during the decision-making process before software is  
distributed.  
Supplementary information (e.g. tutorial collections, best-practice examples, and answers to  
frequently asked questions) is also available in the RSE portal. This information can be used to  
review or assess your own software project and to prepare for a personal advisory session.  
Furthermore, the usual procedures at Forschungszentrum Jülich with regard to software and  
common open source licences as well as their legal classification, which are described in the  
aforementioned best-practice examples, can be used as a decision-making aid.  
4.2 Support and training services  
Training courses on software engineering play an important role in enabling early-career scientists  
to develop good software and in implementing the standards for good software at Jülich.  
Forschungszentrum Jülich would therefore like to provide easy access to internal and external  
training opportunities. Furthermore, the existing range of training courses on software development  
methodologies and best practices at Forschungszentrum Jülich will be further expanded in the future.  
The RSE portal lists internal and external services and information, which are regularly updated and  
continuously added to. This includes information on the activities and courses offered by the  
Helmholtz Association such as the HIFIS Software Helpdesk, training courses offered by HIFIS and  
HIDA, the Helmholtz Open Science Forum Research Software, and services offered by RWTH  
Aachen University (e.g. within the framework of JARA-CSD). In addition to and in close cooperation  
with the RSE portal, a virtual workspace for software developers with corresponding services will be  
set up on Forschungszentrum Jülich’s learning platform (JuLearn).

4.3 Scientific and economic exploitation and choice of licences  
Forschungszentrum Jülich supports the publication of software as open source software to help  
strengthen “open science” and thus to achieve a more effective and open exchange of information  
within science and to promote the transfer of findings to society.  
In cases where economic exploitation of software is possible, makes sense, and is in the interest of

elements of a coordinated software exploitation strategy, thus safeguarding the interests of the

4.5 Proprietary licences  
If economic exploitation of the software or a more individual licence is required – e.g. because the  
distribution of the code or the type of use of the software (e.g. commercial use) should be restricted  
– Forschungszentrum Jülich recommends granting a proprietary licence.  
Contractual models are also possible, where the software is licensed proprietarily but free of charge  
for academic purposes. Commercial use, however, is subject to a fee [16]. If software is licensed in  
combination with know-how (e.g. a building plan), proprietary licensing should always be chosen.  
If the aim is to freely share and modify software only within a closed community, proprietary licences  
are the best option since an existing open source licence contradicts a closed community. In the  
case of joint software development in large communities where the parties also contribute  
background software to the collaboration, it is essential to take licence compatibility into account.  
Software that is covered by an open source licence may also be commercially exploited if  
Forschungszentrum Jülich is the copyright holder. Various models can be pursued regarding the  
exploitation of open source software. If software is contributed to cooperation projects as a  
background IP, it must be assigned a licence before being distributed to partners, as software may  
not be distributed without granting explicit rights of use (open source or proprietary), even in  
collaborations.

rights of third parties are violated and thus a scientific or economic use or reuse is restricted or even  
prevented.  
At Forschungszentrum Jülich, UE-I is responsible for drafting and negotiating any licensing  
agreements that grant proprietary use. If proprietary licensing is desired, possible licensing  
conditions, contractual framework conditions, the IP situation, etc. are discussed together with the  
institute and a corresponding licensing agreement is drawn up. To this end, UE-I consults with Law  
(R-R) regarding the contractual framework conditions and negotiates the licensing agreement with  
the potential licensee.

The implementation of these software guidelines at Forschungszentrum Jülich provides employees  
with a practical and secure framework within which they can develop and distribute software that  
meets high quality standards.  
By assigning software or software projects to application classes, identifying quality standards as  
well as corresponding recommended measures, considering possible transfer paths or types of  
licences and the associated legal aspects, professionalization can be achieved across the board at  
Forschungszentrum Jülich.  
This is supplemented by Forschungszentrum Jülich’s decision-making aids and advisory services

In addition, Forschungszentrum Jülich would like to expand its existing range of internal training  
courses on software development methodologies and best practices in the future to enable early-  
career scientists to achieve high standards in software development and documentation.

6 Explanation of terms  
Application class:  
Application classes17 serve to classify software and set out corresponding rules and recommendations  
regarding appropriate software development practice and documentation. They facilitate the checking of the  
rules and communication on the related topics. Further information on this concept can be found in the “DLR  
Software Engineering Guidelines” (from page 7)18, which provide an exemplary definition of sequential  
application classes. “Application Class 1”, for example, specifies minimal recommendations for non-critical  
research software of limited scope (e.g. data analysis scripts) and is compatible with the minimal development  
and documentation practice specified in that document.  
Archive/Archiving:  
Archiving software programmed at Forschungszentrum Jülich contributes to the sustainability of software  
development. The primary aim of archiving is to secure versions for verification, provenance, and  
reproducibility purposes in the context of scientific results.  
Archiving is carried out including associated data (e.g. metadata, documentation, and, if applicable, runtime  
environment and test data). In addition, retention periods must be adhered to in accordance with the rules of  
good scientific practice in the disciplines.  
Options for archiving, depending on requirements and application class, may include repositories (Zenodo,  
Jülich DATA, etc....), software journals, and the Software Heritage archive. More detailed information can be  
found in the best practices and in the RSE portal.  
Sustainable software requires that even inactive or archived software remains reusable. The fast pace of  
technologies in the software environment poses a challenge with regard to software archiving. When planning,  
implementing, and exploiting self-programmed software, software developers, project managers, and senior  
employees must jointly decide on how to act in a specific instance based on the application class. The  
corresponding (long-term) resources for this must be considered by the project managers from the beginning.  
Code Repository:  
A code repository is an archive of the code that is being worked on. Beyond the code itself, you can keep  
things such as documentation, notes, web pages, and other items in your repository. A code repository is  
required for any successful software development project.  
Contributor agreement:  
A contributor agreement or contributor licence agreement (CLA) is a document describing the terms and  
conditions under which intellectual property can be contributed to a project; usually this is a software project  
under an open source licence. Forschungszentrum Jülich provides a verified model agreement in the RSE  
portal, which can be presented to external contributors.  
Copyright:  
Software is subject to copyright. Section 69a of the Act on Copyright and Related Rights (UrhG) grants  
protection to software of any form, including design material, which applies to any form of expression (QC, C,  
EXE, modules). However, ideas and principles which underlie the work are not protected.  
Although moral rights cannot be transferred according to German law, extensive rights of use and agreements  
on exploitation rights are permissible. If American law applies, for example, then the entire copyright can be  
transferred.  
Once the software developers are in an employment relationship with Forschungszentrum Jülich, the rights of  
use and exploitation are automatically held by Forschungszentrum Jülich (details are set out in Section 69b of

When using or integrating code, rights must be granted by the creator or their employer if the right to use the

This also applies if only program components or partial sequences are integrated into your own software. In  
this case, an intensive examination is required to see to what extent the various licences of the third-party  
software to be integrated contain incompatible regulations. This may mean that your own software cannot be  
published under the intended open source licence. In addition, the open source licence of the software used  
may expire if the licensing conditions are not complied with.

As soon as persons from countries outside the EU or equivalent countries (UK, USA, JP,...) are involved in the  
project, an assessment of the project under foreign trade legislation may be required. You can find out whether  
this is necessary for your project using a checklist in the RSE portal.  
Git:  
Git is a version control system that allows you to track who made what code changes and when. Git provides  
options for easily updating a shared or public code version on GitHub (https://github.com/).  
Information security:  
The Data Protection and Information Security Unit provides a classification system for the level of protection  
of data in regulations on information security (in preparation). When creating software, it is therefore  
recommended to consider the classification of incorporated data and the software itself according to the  
regulations at an early stage. A classification must be provided at the latest when substantial code parts are  
available.  
Licences:  
OSS licences: The general view is that the open source definition (http://www.opensource.org/docs/osd)  
correctly reflects the open source community’s understanding of the term “open source software”. The  
decisive criterion of the definition is that a software licence allows the comprehensive “free” use of the  
program and does not provide for any restriction of the licensees or areas of use, which means that it may be  
used by anyone for any purpose. The absence of royalties and the openly accessible source code are  
essential prerequisites for free usability. Therefore, the decisive factor is always the licensing agreement  
under which software is made available. (https://www.ifross.org)  
Proprietary licences:  
The essential difference between proprietary and open source licences is that proprietary licences can be  
designed completely freely and, in particular, open up the possibility of fully restricting the distribution of code.  
Although the use of an open source licence does not generally impose a distribution obligation, this right cannot  
be restricted vis-à-vis a third party. Furthermore, the open source licence only applies if the third party  
distributes the software. For example, offering a service using the software without distributing it would be  
possible without any problems. Therefore, a proprietary licence is of interest if you want to restrict the  
distribution of the code itself or a certain type of use (commercial use).  
Metadata:  
Metadata are data about data: any data that is stored alongside your primary data and that adds to the  
understanding of its content and context is metadata. Good metadata describes the content of a dataset as  
well as the context of how it was gathered and analysed. They are part of the documentation of research  
processes and results necessary to comply with quality standards.  
Intrinsic metadata describes properties inherent to the data they describe. This metadata may be, amongst  
others, technical metadata, such as information about the file size, format, data type, or storage location.

Software:  
In the context of these guidelines, software refers to all forms of program code (e.g. source code together with  
associated documentation, parameters, and workflows) and executable programs generated from it, which are  
developed and/or (re)used in the context of a science-related activity at Helmholtz centres.  
Software life cycle:  
The life cycle of software describes all essential development stages from the idea, conception, development,  
use, and maintenance to archiving and decommissioning.  
Software Project:  
A software project is a complex undertaking by two or more persons within the boundaries of time, budget,  
and staff resources that produces new or enhanced computer code.  
Software publication:  
Analogous to research data and scientific texts, software is also an outcome of scientific work. Thus, as a  
scientific publication, it can be made known to a wide audience. Nonetheless, analogous to research data,  
access to software from the publication may be restricted. Like research data and text publications, a software  
publication includes metadata by means of which it is findable and citable.  
Standards/current state of the art:  
The term “state of the art” is a legal and technical term, which implies certain minimum requirements for the  
product. The Manual for Drafting Legislation by the German Federal Ministry of Justice states the following:  
“The state of the art refers to the level of development of advanced procedures, facilities and modes of  
operation which, according to prevailing opinion among leading experts, suggest that achievement of the  
statutory objective is guaranteed. Procedures, facilities and modes of operation or comparable procedures,  
facilities and modes of operation must have proven their worth in practice or should – if this is not yet the  
case – have been successfully tested in normal operation where possible.”

Version control system:  
When developing high-quality software, it is important to use version control systems to keep track of all code  
changes and the software developers involved. These systems must be linked to collaborative functions for  
projects and communities. The version control system serves to record changes to data (e.g. documents,  
program code) by a defined group of persons that are managed in a common storage location (“repository”).  
Each change is associated with a timestamp and the person who made the change. The resulting history  
allows changes to be traced and enables a return to earlier stages.  
Forschungszentrum Jülich currently offers such a collaborative platform for its employees (e.g. currently JuGIT,  
as of 2021). Furthermore, it is desirable that this platform can be used for national and international cooperation  
by integrating established identity federations such as currently (2021) eduGain, DFN AAI, and/or the  
Helmholtz AAI, which allows use by those who are not employed by Forschungszentrum Jülich in this sense.  
Moreover, similar platforms may be offered for closed groups or for other important reasons. Platforms  
provided by the Helmholtz Association within the framework of HIFIS or established global platforms such as  
GitHub.com or GitLab.com are also available and can be used at the joint discretion of developers, senior  
employees, and project managers, for example to increase reach and visibility.

Before software developed at the Forschungszentrum Jülich can be distributed and thereby

Passing on to third parties  
When choosing a license, it is primarily a matter of which rights and obligations the licensee  
is to be subject to. The spectrum ranges from e.g. MIT (all rights, hardly any obligations) to  
GPL v3 (limited rights, obligations when passing on).  
If it is important in the project that as many partners as possible get uncomplicated and fast

without obligations.

distributed under restrictions, then obligations must be imposed on the licensees (e.g. the  
passing on of the source code).
